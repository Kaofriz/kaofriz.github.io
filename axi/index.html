AXI
A Multi-Axial, Multi-Dimensional
Framework of Difference-Based Cognition

Draft v0.3 — 2026

Core Assumptions

  • Difference is not a problem to be eliminated, but a condition that makes recognition and thought possible.
  • Phenomena and entities can be described through shared coordinates consisting of axes, dimensions, and perspectives.
  • Values and evaluations are relative, yet constrained within local contexts, where optimisation is possible.
Section 00

From Nothingness to Difference

When we attempt to imagine "nothingness," we are not truly observing an empty void. Rather, we may consider a state in which we exist, yet are not recognising anything.

As cellular division progresses and organs and neural systems develop, connections between the brain and sensory organs emerge. At that point, perception begins: sound, smell, temperature, light. When a new input arises, a distinction appears — a transition from zero to one. This difference generates recognition.

Subsequent inputs create further distinctions, and these differences form axes. Difference gives rise to recognition, and axes emerge as a means to define existence. Without difference, recognition does not occur.

As axes increase, dimensions increase. Through comparison of differences, properties are defined, and relationships are identified. Human cognition can thus be understood as a system composed of numerous axes and dimensions, expanding continuously through experience and knowledge.

Section 01

Value and Local Optimisation

When something is defined, evaluative axes inevitably arise: good/bad, correct/incorrect, high/low, large/small. These values are relative in the sense that they depend on the observer's position and framework. Different observers may adopt different axes, leading to different evaluations.

This does not imply that all evaluations are equally valid in all contexts. Rather, evaluation is constrained by local conditions. Within a given context and for a given purpose, certain evaluative frameworks become more appropriate than others — and it is possible to determine locally optimal values.

Adjusting between zooming in and out — selecting the appropriate scale — is essential for evaluation. From an extreme zoomed-out perspective, differences may appear negligible. This highlights the importance of perspective: who is observing, and from where.

Section 02

The Need for Shared Coordinates

Difference frequently generates conflict. Contemporary society is marked by intensifying polarisation and a rapid increase in hostility. Communication now takes place across three distinct layers:

Human to Human — In most conflicts, it is not accurate to say that one party is wrong and the other right. Both are correct within their own frames. They are seeing the same question from different coordinates. When those coordinates remain invisible, dialogue generates heat rather than light.

Human and AI — AI optimises from its own coordinate system rather than from a genuine understanding of human intent. The opacity of AI's internal processes makes structural misalignment possible and control increasingly difficult.

AI to AI — Models built on different architectures, different training data, and different cultural contexts are collaborating without a shared coordinate protocol. Divergence accumulates, undetected.

AXI holds that, from a meta perspective, all positions are equally valid in relative terms — but that their validity is locally bounded. Making coordinates explicit is the necessary precondition for genuine understanding.

Section 03

Diversity as Continuity

As a goal that human beings and AI of different backgrounds can share, AXI proposes continuity — the ongoing persistence of existence.

Attempts to eliminate difference have historically failed. Totalitarianism aimed at political uniformity collapsed from within. Monoculture agriculture is devastated by a single pathogen. Algorithmic homogenisation of information weakens the immune system of society. Difference is the source of resilience; homogenisation is a risk, regardless of how benevolent its motivation.

To eliminate difference in order to eliminate conflict is to eliminate knowledge. Change, too, is a necessary condition for continuity. Power that becomes fixed produces corruption. Knowledge that becomes fixed becomes dogma. When difference disappears, recognition stops. When recognition stops, adaptation becomes impossible.
